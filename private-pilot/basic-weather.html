Basic Weather

Interpreting weather as a beginning pilot

Private Pilot
Getting Started
Author

Charath Ranganathan

Published

May 5, 2024

As a beginning pilot, you rely on your instructor to tell you when it is safe to fly. Often, your instructor will tell you that the weather is not great and your lesson is canceled. Sometimes, you look at the weather forecasts and wonder whether your lesson will be canceled or whether you should make the trek to your airport for a lesson.

After a few lessons (typically 2 - 4), I like to introduce weather reports to my students and train them to make a determination of whether they think it is safe to fly. This orients them towards aeronautical decision making (ADM), teaches them about personal minimums, and familiarizes them with concepts that are critical as they progress toward their first solo.

Weather Minimums

A weather minimum, as the name indicates, is the lowest acceptable set of weather conditions under which a pilot will still fly.

Weather minimums are an essential aspect of aeronautical decision making, and safe pilots will have their own weather minimums incorporated into their personal minimums – a set of minimums that include the aircraft’s condition, the pilot’s wellbeing, and the weather – which a safe pilot uses to determine whether to undertake a mission or not.

Components of Weather Minimums

  1. Visibility: How far can the pilot see while in the air. Expressed in statute miles.
  2. Ceiling: How high above the ground are the bases of the clouds. Expressed in feet.
  3. Winds:
    • Speed: How strong are the winds. Expressed in knots.
    • Direction: Are the winds directly down the runway or is there a significant crosswind component?
    • Gusts: Are the winds steady or are there gusts?
  4. Adverse Conditions: These would include things like turbulence, thunderstorms, icing, etc. They’re not as relevant for flying in SoCal (since we hardly get any real weather), but they’re important to keep in mind.

Weather Minimums for A Pre-solo Student

For a pre-solo student, I like to keep things fairly “easy”. As the student gets closer to solo, I add more challenging situations in order to help the student understand (appreciate?) how even small changes in the weather could make their flights “interesting”.

  • Visibility: 5 SM. Can be 3 SM if staying in the pattern at KEMT.
  • Ceiling: 3,500 feet. Can be 1,500 feet if staying in the pattern at KEMT.
  • Winds: 5 knots. As we progress toward solo, this will increase to 10 knots, with a crosswind component of 2 knots.
  • Adverse Conditions: No turbulence greater than light. Obviously, no thunderstorms in the vicinity. Light rain or rain showers are OK – they are irritating during preflight and can make landing a little challenging.

Weather Reports

There are two weather reports that are relevant to our discussion:

  1. TAF: The “terminal aerodrome forecast” which is issued every 6 hours (4 times a day) and valid for either 24 or 30 hours. The TAF, as the name implies, is a forecast.
  2. METAR: The “routine aviation meteorological report”. This report is issued once an hour or more often if conditions change significantly. They are an observation, i.e. a point-in-time record of the weather at an airport.

Obtaining Weather Reports

The easiest way to obtain weather reports is through an EFB like Foreflight. But, if you do not have access to Foreflight, you can find all weather reports at the Aviation Weather Center.

Interpreting Weather Reports

Let us understand how to interpret these weather reports with a practical example.

On May 3rd, around 17:00 local, I was trying to determine whether I would be able to fly with a student on May 4th at 15:00 local (22:00Z at this time of year).

Since my home airport, KEMT, does not have a TAF, one has to infer the possible weather around KEMT by “triangulating” the weather from a few nearby airports for whom TAFs are generated.

Typically, we look at a combination of TAFs from KLGB, KBUR, and KONT to determine what the weather at KEMT might be.

So, let’s take a look at what the TAFs looked like at 17:00 local on the 3rd.

Let’s look at the TAF for Long Beach airport (KLGB) and analyze it.

The first line shows the following information:

  • The airport for which the TAF was issued (KLGB).
  • The time it was issued (032331Z). This means the TAF was issued on the 3rd of the month at 2331Z.
  • The date and time for which the TAF is valid (0400/0424). This means that the TAF is valid from 00Z on the 4th (0400) through 24Z on the 4th (0424).
  • The general weather to be expected during the valid period:
    • 22008KT: Winds from 220 (the south west) at 8 knots. Winds are expressed in a 5-digit segment, with the wind direction being the first 3 digits and the wind speed being the last 2 digits.
    • P6SM: Visibility of greater than 6 statute miles (the “P” stands for “Plus”)
    • BKN028: Sky conditions: A broken layer of clouds at 2,800 feet AGL.
